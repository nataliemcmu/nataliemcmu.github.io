About me, Natalie

My name is Natalie and I'm a trans woman from North Yorkshire. I first started listening to shoegaze music while studying music and sound recording at university in York when I was recommended the album 'EP's 1988-1991 and rare tracks' by My Bloody Valentine by a friend. There was something so cathartic about the sound of the songs. The undulating noise with soft etherial vocals felt new and lovely to me. I especially loved the song 'off your face', it just made me feel whole.

Unfortunately, due to the covid pandemic, my degree in music was cut short, and I instead opted to go down the route of maths and physics. In many ways, this was a blessing, as it meant my time playing and recording music was all to myself. I could be selfish and do what I really wanted to do.

I'd been playing guitar for around 4 years by this point. I'd picked guitar up after buying a beaten up Â£4 classical from a charity shop in my hometown. I was 14, and has been learning classical piano since the age of 6, and quite frankly, it was boring now. Listening to bands like slowdive, my bloody valentine, julie, ringo deathstar and computerwife at university really shaped how I played guitar.

I think something that doesn't get talked about enough is the harmony of shoegaze. Everyone talks about texture, but one of the fundamental elements to the texture of shoegaze is the use of extended chords, and I think this is something that quite a few new-age shoegazers miss out on.

Since finished university I have continued to play and record music, although I am yet to release any of my own compositions, just covers. One thing I really enjoy about the recording process is just messing around and taking risks, although this mindset has made me a bit of a perfectionist when it comes to how I want things to sound. I sometimes feel like a child painting, not knowing when to put down the brush and eventually the paper is just a mess of colours.

Recently I've been living with my partner without my electric guitar, and I tried creating a swirly, distorted sound with my acoustic - I think it turned out quite well. One thing I love about making covers is producing the cover photo - not that it really matters to anyone but me. I've always been inspired by music magazines and press photos, especially those from the 90's, and try to capture that in my cover photos.
